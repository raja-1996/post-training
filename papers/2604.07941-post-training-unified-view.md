# Large Language Model Post-Training: A Unified View of Off-Policy and On-Policy Learning

**arxiv:** 2604.07941 (v2, 16 Apr 2026)
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.07941
**Authors:** Shiwan Zhao (Nankai Univ.), Zhihu Wang (Huawei), Xuyang Zhao, Jiaming Zhou, Caiyue Xu (Huawei), Chenfei Liu (Huawei), Liting Zhang, Yuhang Jia, Yanzhe Zhang, Hualong Yu, Zichen Xu, Qicheng Li, Yong Qin (Nankai Univ.)
**Pages:** 38

**One-line summary:** A conceptual survey that argues LLM post-training is best organized not by objective family (SFT vs. DPO vs. PPO ...) but by **trajectory provenance** (off-policy vs. on-policy) plus **supervision interface** (token targets, preferences, rewards, verifiers, teacher-guided targets) plus three **functional roles** — *effective support expansion*, *policy reshaping*, and *behavioral consolidation* — which together explain why modern frontier systems combine multiple regimes in multi-stage pipelines rather than relying on any single objective.

## Key framing: Off-Policy vs On-Policy

The paper's central organizational claim is that the most consequential distinction in post-training is **not the algebraic form of the loss** but the **source of the trajectories on which optimization actually occurs**:

- **Off-policy learning** — updates are driven primarily by **externally supplied trajectories** (instruction–response pairs, expert demonstrations, paired/ranked preference data, verified solutions, synthetic teacher outputs, replayed traces from earlier policies). Representative families: SFT, offline preference optimization, off-policy distillation/teacher-guided transfer.
- **On-policy learning** — updates are driven primarily by **rollouts sampled from the current policy** with feedback (rewards, online preference judgments, constitutional rules, tests, verifiers, teacher corrections) computed on those freshly generated trajectories. Representative families: RLHF, RLAIF, RLVR, online preference optimization, on-policy process supervision/verifier guidance, iterative refinement/self-improvement, on-policy teacher-guided transfer.

This is *related to but distinct from* the offline/online RL distinction: offline/online RL is about whether a fixed dataset is used vs. continuing environment interaction; here off-policy/on-policy is about **whether training trajectories come from external sources or from the learner itself**.

Three layered analytical questions structure the rest of the survey:
1. **Where does learning occur?** (provenance — top-level)
2. **How is supervision attached?** (interface — secondary descriptive layer)
3. **What behavioral change or systems role does the stage serve?** (functional role)

### Supervision interfaces (secondary layer)

Within either provenance regime, supervision can be attached through:

- **Token targets** (e.g. teacher-forced next-token loss in SFT)
- **Pairwise / ranked preferences** (e.g. DPO-style methods)
- **Reward-derived signals** (e.g. RLHF, RLVR)
- **Verifier- or process-based feedback** (verifier-guided methods, process supervision)
- **Teacher-guided targets** (teacher logits, teacher-generated traces, filtered outputs, rationale transfer in distillation)

Importantly, distillation is **not** a third regime parallel to off-policy/on-policy. It is a *teacher-guided supervision interface* that can be instantiated in either regime: off-policy when teacher targets are attached to externally supplied traces; on-policy when teacher targets are attached to learner-generated rollouts.

### Distribution-level functional roles (Sec. 3.3)

Built on an **occupancy-style** view of state visitation, where a state at step *t* is the prompt–prefix pair `s_t = (x, y_<t)` and the action is `a_t = y_t`. The paper defines a normalized state-action occupancy `d^π_{D_x}(s, a)` over prompts sampled from a prompt distribution and rollouts from policy π, plus an effective-support set

```
E_ε(π; D_x) = { (s, a) : d^π_{D_x}(s, a) ≥ ε }
```

— state-action patterns that occur with non-negligible mass. Roles are defined relative to this set:

- **Effective support expansion** — `E_ε(π_1; D_x) \ E_ε(π_0; D_x) ≠ ∅`; training causes previously negligible state-action regions to enter effective support (i.e., new useful behaviors become practically reachable).
- **Policy reshaping** — the dominant change is probability redistribution **within the shared effective support** `C_ε(π_0, π_1; D_x) = E_ε(π_0) ∩ E_ε(π_1)`; better ranking among already-reachable behaviors, calibration, alignment, branch choice.
- **Support attrition** (diagnostic, not a positive role) — `A_ε(π_0, π_1; D_x) = E_ε(π_0; D_x) \ E_ε(π_1; D_x)`; previously effective patterns drop out of effective support, capturing catastrophic forgetting / regression.

### Behavioral consolidation (systems-level role, Sec. 3.4)

Distinct from the two distributional roles above, this captures whether useful behavior **survives handoff** across stages, models, or deployment settings. Sub-forms: **retention** (across stages), **transfer** (teacher → student, search → policy), **amortization** (compiling expensive runtime processes like reflection/search/multi-pass verification into a cheaper direct policy), **compression** (distilling into a smaller deployment model). Distillation is the prototypical instantiation, though replay and model merging can also serve this role.

### Operational heuristic table (Table 2 of the paper)

| Dominant role | Typical positive evidence | Question answered | Counter-evidence | Attrition risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Expansion | Newly occupied effective regions; reachability of useful prefixes / actions; emergence of previously absent solution patterns | Does training make previously inaccessible behavior reachable? | Gains concentrated on already-reachable responses/styles | Previously effective regions may shrink |
| Reshaping | Better ranking, better local branch choice, stronger calibration without widened support | Does training improve choice within already-available behavior? | Improvements require importing genuinely new trajectories | Harmful reweighting may degrade prior behavior |
| Consolidation | Preservation across stage transitions; successful student transfer; robust deployment without scaffold | Can useful behavior be preserved/transferred/compressed/amortized? | Gains exist only with teacher/search/scaffold present | Stage transitions may erase induced behavior |

## All post-training approaches catalogued

### 1. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) — Off-policy, token targets (Sec. 4.1)

- **Vanilla SFT / instruction tuning** [Wei et al. FLAN, Sanh et al. T0, Wang et al. Super-NaturalInstructions, Chung et al. Flan-T5]. Teacher-forced next-token loss on instruction–response pairs.
- **Self-generated instruction data**: SELF-INSTRUCT, Alpaca [Wang et al. 2023; Taori et al. 2023].
- **LIMA** [Zhou et al. 2023] — Less Is More for Alignment; minimal high-quality SFT.
- **Reasoning-focused SFT**: LIMO [Ye et al.], MetaMath [Yu et al.], MAmmoTH [Yue et al.], MathScale [Tang et al.] — exposing reasoning trace structure that the base model would not reliably generate.
- **Domain SFT**: Med-PaLM-style clinical knowledge tuning [Singhal et al.].
- **SteerLM** [Dong et al.] — attribute-conditioned SFT as a user-steerable alternative to RLHF.

**Functional role** of SFT is *context-dependent*: support-expansion-dominant when demonstrations expose patterns the model couldn't reliably realize on its own (e.g. reasoning SFT, capability bootstrapping); policy-reshaping-dominant when supervision mainly calibrates style/format/alignment over already-latent behavior (e.g. light instruction-format tuning of an already-capable base model).

**Failure modes**: exposure mismatch between teacher-forced training prefixes and learner-rollout prefixes at deployment (the classical scheduled-sampling / DAgger / professor-forcing problem); overfitting to narrow trajectory styles; importing brittle rationales; amplifying superficial regularities when supervision is too narrow.

#### Emerging "model-aware / distribution-aware" SFT variants (Sec. 7.1.1)

The paper highlights a movement away from vanilla likelihood maximization toward SFT objectives or data tailored to the learner's current distribution:

- **PRISM** [Zhao et al. 2026] — disentangles SFT and RL data via gradient concentration.
- **Proximal SFT** [Zhu et al. 2026] — constrains policy drift during SFT.
- **Probability-based objectives for SFT** [Li et al. 2025] — moves beyond log-likelihood for capability-matched supervision.
- **Anchored SFT** [Zhu et al. 2026] — preserves anchoring to base model behavior.
- **Self-Distillation Bridges Distribution Gap** [Yang et al. 2024] — uses model's own outputs to shrink the policy gap before SFT.
- **Mind the Gap / data rewriting for stable off-policy SFT** [Zhao et al. 2025].
- **"Best Instruction-Tuning Data are Those That Fit"** [Zhang/Dai/Peng 2025] — selecting model-fitting responses.
- **Selective Self-to-Supervised Fine-Tuning** [Gupta et al. 2025].
- **SFT generalization with reward rectification** [Wu et al. 2026] — RL-perspective on SFT.

### 2. Offline Preference Optimization — Off-policy, preference interface (Sec. 4.2)

- **DPO (Direct Preference Optimization)** [Rafailov et al. 2023] — the seminal "LLM is secretly a reward model" formulation; pairwise preference loss derived from a Bradley–Terry assumption with implicit KL regularization to a reference policy.
- **ORPO (Monolithic Preference Optimization without Reference Model)** [Hong/Lee/Thorne 2024].
- **CPO (Contrastive Preference Optimization)** [Xu et al. 2024] — for machine translation.
- **KTO (Kahneman–Tversky Optimization / Model Alignment as Prospect Theoretic Optimization)** [Ethayarajh et al. 2024].
- **SimPO (Simple Preference Optimization with a Reference-Free Reward)** [Meng/Xia/Chen 2024].
- **Generalized Preference Optimization (GPO)** [Tang et al. 2024] — unified framework for offline alignment.

**Typical role**: reshaping-dominant. Preference pairs are drawn from candidates the model can already produce, so the gradient mainly redistributes mass within shared effective support (improve ranking, style, calibration, alignment). Preferred examples can occasionally contribute to expansion if they come from a substantially stronger generator and expose new trajectory structure.

**Failure modes**: curated candidate space may miss rollout-dependent failures; offline supervision can over-optimize style while leaving rollout errors uncorrected.

### 3. Off-Policy Teacher-Guided Transfer / Distillation (Sec. 4.3)

- **Classical knowledge distillation** [Hinton/Vinyals/Dean 2015].
- **Learning by Distilling Context** [Snell/Klein/Zhong 2022].
- **In-context Learning Distillation** [Huang et al. 2022].
- **Distilling Step-by-Step** [Hsieh et al. 2023] — outperform larger models with less data through reasoning-trace distillation.
- **Distilling Reasoning Capabilities into Smaller Language Models** [Shridhar/Stolfo/Sachan 2023].
- **Orca / Orca 2** [Mukherjee et al. 2023; Mitra et al. 2023] — progressive learning from explanation traces of GPT-4; teaching small models to reason.
- **MiniLLM** [Gu et al. 2024] — distilling LLMs at scale.

**Typical role**: behavioral *consolidation* — carrying behavior first induced elsewhere (large teacher, RL stage, expensive search/reflection pipeline) into a cheaper, more stable, or more deployable form. **Risk**: incomplete transfer — student matches teacher outputs on the distillation distribution without internalizing what made those outputs reliable.

### 4. RLHF / RLAIF / RLVR — On-policy reward-based (Sec. 5.1)

- **RLHF foundations**: Ziegler et al. 2019 (Fine-Tuning LMs from Human Preferences); Stiennon et al. 2020 (summarization); Bai et al. 2022 (helpful & harmless assistant); Ouyang et al. 2022 (InstructGPT — train LMs to follow instructions with human feedback).
- **Constitutional AI / RLAIF**: Bai et al. 2022 (Constitutional AI — harmlessness from AI feedback); Lee et al. 2024 (RLAIF vs RLHF scaling).
- **RLVR (Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards)**: DeepSeek-R1 [Guo et al. Nature 2025] which incentivizes reasoning via RL with verifiable correctness signals; DeepSeekMath / GRPO [Shao et al. 2024] which introduced Group Relative Policy Optimization for math reasoning.
- **DAPO** [Yu et al. 2025] — open-source LLM RL system at scale (RLVR-style).
- **GSPO (Group Sequence Policy Optimization)** [Zheng et al. 2025].
- **Wen et al. 2026** — RLVR implicitly incentivizes correct reasoning in base LLMs.

**Classical RL background cited**: TRPO [Schulman et al. 2015], PPO [Schulman et al. 2017], Sutton & Barto's RL textbook.

**Typical role**: reshaping-dominant on learner-visited states; can produce *selective* expansion under strong guidance (see §5 emerging directions). **Failure modes**: reward hacking [Skalse et al. 2022], reward-model overoptimization [Gao/Schulman/Hilton 2023], optimization instability [Zheng et al. 2023 "Secrets of RLHF Part I: PPO"], catastrophic forgetting from large updates [Sabbaghi et al. 2026 "Robust Policy Optimization to Prevent Catastrophic Forgetting"].

### 5. Online Preference Optimization (Sec. 5.2)

Keeps the preference-based supervision interface but moves data refresh toward the current policy.

- **Calandriello et al. 2024** — Human alignment via online preference optimization.
- **Online DPO with Fast–Slow Chasing** [Qi et al. 2024].
- **The Crucial Role of Samplers in Online DPO** [Shi/Zhou/Du 2025].
- **Online Preference Alignment via Count-Based Exploration** [Bai et al. 2025].

**Role**: still reshaping-dominant, but a bridge between offline preference optimization and full on-policy RL.

### 6. On-Policy Process Supervision and Verifier-Guided Correction (Sec. 5.3)

Feedback attached to **intermediate** learner states rather than only terminal rewards.

- **"Let's Verify Step by Step"** [Lightman et al. 2024] — foundational process reward modeling work (cites OpenAI's PRM).
- **Math-Shepherd** [Wang et al. 2024] — verify & reinforce step-by-step without human annotation.
- **Rewarding Progress: Scaling Automated Process Verifiers** [Setlur et al. 2025].
- **Reward-SQL** [Zhang et al. 2025] — stepwise reasoning + process-supervised rewards for text-to-SQL.
- **Process Supervision-Guided Policy Optimization for Code Generation** [Dai et al. 2024].
- **AgentPRM** [Xi et al. 2025] — process reward models for LLM agents.

Cited general verifier survey: Guan et al. "Search, Verify and Feedback" [arXiv 2411.11504]; Zheng et al. survey of process reward models [2510.08049].

**Role**: fine-grained learner-state reshaping (denser credit assignment than terminal reward). **Risk**: verifier mismatch, scalability, reachable-state dependence (only visited branches can be corrected); proxy overoptimization.

### 7. Iterative Refinement / Self-Improvement Loops (Sec. 5.4)

Critique–revise loops, reasoning self-training, self-correction pipelines:

- **STaR (Bootstrapping Reasoning With Reasoning)** [Zelikman et al. 2022].
- **ReST (Reinforced Self-Training)** [Gulcehre et al. 2023].
- **ReST-MCTS\*** [Zhang et al. 2024] — LLM self-training with process-reward-guided tree search.
- **Beyond Human Data (ReST-EM-style scaling self-training)** [Singh et al. 2024].
- **Critique-GRPO** [Zhang et al. 2025] — natural language + numerical feedback.
- **S²R** [Ma et al. 2025] — teach LLMs to self-verify and self-correct via RL.
- **Self-Correct via RL** [Kumar et al. 2025].
- **Language Model Self-Improvement by RL Contemplation** [Pang et al. 2024].
- **Fixing Distribution Shifts of LLM Self-Critique via On-Policy Self-Play** [Bao et al. 2025].

**Role**: reshaping-dominant. **Risk**: weak critique reinforces superficial patterns; gains may depend on the loop and fail to transfer to a final deployed policy.

### 8. On-Policy Teacher-Guided Transfer (Sec. 5.5 & 7.1.5)

Teacher targets/corrections attached to learner-generated rollouts (vs. teacher-curated traces in the off-policy variant).

- **On-Policy Distillation of Language Models** [Agarwal et al. 2024] — learning from self-generated mistakes.
- **Black-Box On-Policy Distillation** [Ye et al. 2025].
- **On-Policy Context Distillation** [Ye et al. 2026].
- **Self-Distilled Reasoner** [Zhao et al. 2026] — on-policy self-distillation.
- **Learning beyond Teacher: Generalized On-Policy Distillation with Reward Extrapolation** [Yang et al. 2026].
- **CRISP** [Sang et al. 2026] — compressed reasoning via iterative self-policy distillation.

**Role**: usually reshaping-dominant learner-state correction; can also support selective expansion when teacher steers rollouts into previously unreachable downstream regions. Connects to behavioral consolidation when on-policy gains are later distilled forward. **Risks**: teacher cost, limited teacher advantage, incomplete retention after teacher removal.

### 9. Guided On-Policy Learning (rollout-time hints/scaffolds, Sec. 7.1.2)

Methods that guide on-policy learning via rollout-time hints, self-explanations, templates, scaffolds, structured prompting:

- **Self-Hinting Language Models** [Liao et al. 2026].
- **ExPO** [Zhou et al. 2025] — self-explanation-guided RL for hard reasoning.
- **TemplateRL** [Wu et al. 2025] — structured template-guided RL for reasoning.
- **HiPO** [Deng et al. 2026] — self-hint policy optimization for RLVR.
- **Rubric-Scaffolded RL** [Zhou et al. 2025] — breaking the exploration bottleneck.

These can selectively expand effective support by altering the rollout distribution so the learner repeatedly encounters behavior it would not realize under its unaided policy.

### 10. Interleaved / Unified SFT–RL Training (Sec. 6.2, 7.1.3)

Softening the SFT→RL handoff by interleaving or unifying off-policy supervision with on-policy reshaping:

- **Interleaved Online Fine-Tuning for Hardest Questions** [Ma et al. 2026].
- **Learning to Reason under Off-Policy Guidance** [Yan et al. 2025].
- **SRFT** [Fu et al. 2026] — single-stage Supervised + Reinforcement Fine-Tuning.
- **UFT (Unifying Supervised and Reinforcement Fine-Tuning)** [Liu/Farina/Ozdaglar 2025].
- **Dynamic Weighting of SFT + RL** [Zhang et al. 2026] — on-policy RL meets off-policy experts.
- **Self-Optimized Alignment** [Wang et al. 2025] — learning to align, aligning to learn.
- **RePO (Replay-Enhanced Policy Optimization)** [Li et al. 2025] — softens the interleave by replaying.

### 11. Multi-stage / Pipeline Recipes (Sec. 6.2, 7.1.4, 7.1.6)

The paper highlights five recurring composition patterns (its Table 5):

| Pattern | Early "support" stage | Reshaping stage | Consolidation stage |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SFT → preference optimization** | SFT exposes useful traces | Preference reweighting | Often implicit |
| **SFT → RLHF / RLVR** | SFT seeds initial support | On-policy RL on learner rollouts | Optional later transfer |
| **Interleaved SFT–RL** | Continuous off-policy supply | Simultaneous on-policy correction | Often implicit |
| **SFT → RL → distillation** | External supervision seeds capability | RL/search refines under rollout | Distillation amortizes into deployable student |
| **Search/verifier-heavy induction → policy transfer** | Search/filtering/verifier-heavy procedure induces costly behavior | Optional learner-state correction | Distillation/policy transfer compresses into reusable policy |

**Cited frontier-model recipes that instantiate these patterns**:
- **DeepSeek-R1** [Guo et al. Nature 2025] — multi-stage SFT + GRPO RLVR + distillation pipeline.
- **Llama 3 Herd** [Grattafiori et al. 2024] — SFT + DPO + reward modeling stages.
- **Tulu 3** [Lambert et al. 2025] — frontier open-recipe combining SFT, DPO, RLVR.
- **Kimi K2** [2025] — agentic intelligence post-training.
- **DeepSeek-V3.2** [Liu et al. 2025] — frontier model with multi-stage pipeline.
- **GLM-5** [Zeng et al. 2026] — from "vibe coding" to "agentic engineering".
- **MiMo-V2-Flash** [Xiao et al. 2026].
- **Nemotron-Cascade 2** [Yang et al. 2026] — Cascade RL + multi-domain on-policy distillation.
- **AgentArk** [Luo et al. 2026] — distill multi-agent intelligence into single LLM agent.
- **Project Aletheia** [Dixit/Liang/Telang 2026] — verifier-guided distillation of backtracking.
- **Learning from Committee** [Li et al. 2025] — reasoning distillation from a mixture of teachers.

Other consolidation / transfer mechanisms cited:
- **InsCL** [Wang et al. 2024] — replay-based continual learning for instruction-tuning.
- **MergeBench** [He et al. 2025] — model merging benchmark.

### 12. Capability boundary diagnostics

- **"The Invisible Leash: Why RLVR May or May Not Escape Its Origin"** [Wu et al. 2025] — empirical support / pass@k as a proxy to test whether RLVR expands capability beyond the base model or only improves selection.
- **"Does RL Really Incentivize Reasoning Capacity Beyond the Base Model?"** [Yue et al. 2025].

These connect to the open problem of distinguishing genuine **support expansion** from improved **elicitation** within already-latent capability.

## Notable formulations / equations

The paper is conceptual rather than mathematical, but key equations are:

**Autoregressive policy factorization** (Eq. 1):
```
π_θ(y | x) = ∏_{t=1}^T π_θ(y_t | x, y_<t)
```

**Normalized state-action occupancy** (Eq. 2):
```
d^{π_θ}_{D_x}(s, a) = E_{x~D_x, y~π_θ(·|x)} [ Σ_{t=1}^{T(y)} η_t(y) · 1{s_t = s, a_t = a} ]
```
with weights `η_t(y) ≥ 0`, `Σ_t η_t(y) = 1` (uniform `η_t = 1/T(y)` is the simplest choice).

**State occupancy** (Eq. 3): `d^π_{D_x}(s) = Σ_a d^π_{D_x}(s, a)`.

**Effective support** (Eq. 5):
```
E_ε(π; D_x) = { (s, a) : d^π_{D_x}(s, a) ≥ ε }
```

**Effective support expansion criterion** (Eq. 6):
```
E_ε(π_1; D_x) \ E_ε(π_0; D_x) ≠ ∅
```

**Shared (reshaping) support** (Eq. 7):
```
C_ε(π_0, π_1; D_x) = E_ε(π_0; D_x) ∩ E_ε(π_1; D_x)
```

**Support attrition** (Eq. 8):
```
A_ε(π_0, π_1; D_x) = E_ε(π_0; D_x) \ E_ε(π_1; D_x)
```

**Generic post-training optimization template** (Eq. 4):
```
max_θ  E_{(x, y, z) ~ D}  [ J(θ; x, y, z) ]
```
where `D` is any supervision-bearing tuple source, `y` is an output/trajectory, and `z` is additional structure (preferences, rewards, verifier outcomes, critiques, teacher guidance). The paper deliberately uses this single template to show that off-policy SFT, DPO, PPO/GRPO RLVR, distillation, etc., all instantiate it — what differs is **where `D` comes from** (provenance), **how `z` is structured** (interface), and **what behavioral role the resulting stage plays**.

The paper does **not** present method-specific loss equations (e.g. it does not write out the DPO log-sigmoid loss, the PPO clipped surrogate, or the GRPO group-relative advantage). It cites these methods but treats them as instances of the unified template.

## Comparisons / takeaways from the paper

The paper's comparison tables (Tables 3, 4, 6, 7) condense its core analytical claims:

**Off-policy regime — when to use it** (Sec. 4.5): when useful behavior must be externally introduced, broadly established, or efficiently reshaped on curated data. Strong for early behavior establishment, broad off-policy support, offline quality control, and consolidation. Structural blind spot: **learner-state mismatch** — does not directly optimize the states the model later visits at deployment, so failures depending on self-generated branching/compounding errors remain under-corrected.

**On-policy regime — when to use it** (Sec. 5.7): when the main bottleneck is failure on learner-generated states. Can directly observe compounding reasoning errors, bad branch selection, weak recovery. Structural blind spot: **does not by itself reliably import useful behavior outside current effective support**. If desirable behaviors rarely appear in learner rollouts, on-policy correction has little to reinforce.

**Why hybrids are natural** (Sec. 6.1): different bottlenecks dominate at different points — useful behavior may first need to be brought into reach, then improved under learner rollouts, then preserved/transferred. No single regime addresses all three.

**Comparative recommendations (Sec. 6.5)**:
- *Trajectory provenance* is the most informative top-level axis because objective family alone is too coarse (similar interfaces — preferences, verifiers, teacher guidance — can appear in either regime with very different roles).
- Off-policy methods are *often* better positioned for support expansion; on-policy methods are *more often* reshaping-dominant — but these are tendencies, not strict labels.
- **Off-policy transfer** is stronger when carrying expensive induced behavior into a cheaper student; **on-policy teacher-guided transfer** is stronger when preservation must remain aligned with the learner's own rollout states.
- For multi-stage systems, the central question is not what a method can improve in isolation, but how reliably gains survive later handoff.

**Hybrid pipeline failure modes** (Sec. 6.4):
- **Stage mismatch** — behavior induced under conditions that no longer hold in the next stage.
- **Stage-transition calibration** — too-weak early stage leaves later RL unstable; too-strong/narrow early stage induces brittle inheritance.
- **Gradient interference** — SFT-like and RL-like updates with different dynamics overwrite each other [Yuan et al. 2025 "Mitigating Forgetting Between SFT and RL"; PRISM, Zhao et al. 2026].
- **Transfer attrition** — useful behavior selectively lost during distillation/handoff.
- **Evaluator inconsistency** — multiple evaluators (humans, reward models, verifiers, constitutions, teachers) misaligned across stages.

## Emerging directions (Sec. 7.1)

1. **From vanilla SFT to model-aware / distribution-aware supervision** — redesigning the SFT objective (token reweighting, drift constraint, probability-based losses) or the supervision distribution itself (data selection, self-generated data, trajectory rewriting). See Section 1 above for method list.
2. **From unguided rollouts to guided on-policy learning** — rollout-time hints, self-explanations, templates, rubrics, learned self-correction. See Section 9 above.
3. **From rigid stage separation to interleaved/unified SFT–RL training** — see Section 10 above.
4. **Cross-stage preservation, specialist synthesis, and distillation** — specialist synthesis and cross-stage distillation as systems-level concerns (DeepSeek-R1 distillation step, Nemotron-Cascade 2's multi-domain on-policy distillation, AgentArk, etc.).
5. **From offline compression to on-policy distillation / self-distillation** — see Section 8 above; tightly couples preservation to learner-visited states.
6. **From isolated objectives to adaptive multi-stage systems** — staged combinations of instruction tuning, preference optimization, online RL, tool-use adaptation, deployment-oriented consolidation; growing importance of *agentic* post-training over long horizons with tools.

## Open problems / future directions (Sec. 7.2, summarized in Table 8)

Six clusters:

1. **How should effective support and bottlenecks be diagnosed?** — Define and measure effective support empirically; distinguish whether failures come from missing support, poor learner-state reshaping, or both. Partial progress through completion-level empirical support and large-k pass@k [Wu et al. 2025 "Invisible Leash"; Yue et al. 2025].
2. **How should supervision interfaces be chosen?** — When are token targets, preferences, scalar rewards, verifier judgments, process feedback, or teacher-guided targets most informative for a given bottleneck? Requires controlled comparisons holding provenance + bottleneck fixed while varying only the interface.
3. **What exactly is transferable across stages?** — final answers, reasoning traces, local policies, search behavior, tool-use patterns are potentially distinct objects of consolidation; need explicit transfer ontologies.
4. **How should preservation, forgetting, and handoff fidelity be measured?** — End-task accuracy alone doesn't reveal whether expensive behavior has been stabilized or whether prior competence has been silently lost. Need consolidation-sensitive metrics, stage-handoff studies.
5. **How should multi-stage systems be evaluated?** — Need explicit stage-handoff metrics, cost-aware reporting, separation between scaffolded behavior (with search/verifier active) and amortized deployment behavior.
6. **What mechanisms explain expansion, reshaping, and consolidation?** — Three sub-gaps: (a) genuine expansion vs. better elicitation of latent capability; (b) why different regimes succeed on different bottlenecks; (c) why some induced behaviors are portable and others scaffold-dependent.

## Notes on coverage

The paper is a **conceptual / framework-oriented survey**, not an exhaustive empirical catalogue. It deliberately uses references to anchor each method family rather than write out individual loss equations. Anyone seeking explicit DPO / PPO / GRPO loss formulations or empirical benchmark tables will need to consult the cited primary papers. The taxonomy itself, however, is the contribution — and the paper repeatedly stresses that role assignments are **context-dependent dominant tendencies** rather than fixed labels.
